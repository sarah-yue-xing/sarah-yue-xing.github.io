My career has not followed a straight line, but it has followed a consistent theme: using scientific thinking to solve real-world problems.

I was trained as a researcher in microbiology, environmental engineering, and antimicrobial resistance. My Ph.D. work focused on microbial systems and resistance-related genes, which taught me how to ask research questions, design experiments, interpret messy biological data, and stay comfortable with uncertainty.

After graduate school, I entered the in vitro diagnostics industry. I started from the R&D side, working on assay development, verification and validation, product documentation, and cross-functional technical projects. Over time, my work moved closer to the clinical and regulatory side of diagnostics, including clinical study planning, protocol and CRF development, site coordination, EDC/eTMF setup, and evidence generation for diagnostic products.

I still like research, but I am most drawn to research that is close to real-world use. I am interested in how diagnostic products are evaluated in clinical settings, how study design shapes evidence quality, and how scientific, regulatory, operational, and market considerations come together before a test can meaningfully serve patients.

This website is where I collect my notes, case studies, and reflections on IVD clinical studies, diagnostic evidence generation, regulatory trends, and professional growth.

## Background

I hold a Ph.D. from the University of Illinois Urbana-Champaign with research in Environmental Microbiology and completed postdoctoral research at the University of California, Riverside. I currently work in the IVD industry, with experience across R&D, clinical affairs, and regulatory-facing projects.
