I wrote this post because when I was preparing for the ACRP CCRA® exam, I felt there were not many practical references written from the perspective of someone working on the sponsor side, especially in medical device and IVD clinical studies.

This is not an official exam guide. It is a personal reflection on why I decided to take the exam, how I prepared, what I learned from the process, and what happened on exam day.

## Why I Decided to Take the CCRA Exam

My background has mostly been on the sponsor side. In my work, we often collaborate with CROs to conduct clinical studies for investigational medical devices. Over time, I realized that if I wanted to become stronger in clinical affairs, I needed to understand not only high-level clinical study planning, but also the practical details of clinical trial conduct, site monitoring, documentation, and GCP expectations.

That was the main reason I decided to prepare for the CCRA exam. I wanted to build a more structured understanding of clinical research operations and strengthen my ability to work with CROs, sites, monitors, and internal teams.

For me, the goal was not just to earn a credential. The exam became a way to force myself to study clinical trial conduct more systematically.

## Application Process

I applied for the ACRP certification exam on August 26, 2025. The application required a resume showing relevant clinical research experience. ACRP manually reviewed the application and provided an eligibility decision. In my case, the review was very quick, and I received eligibility confirmation within one day.

After that, I scheduled my first exam for September 27, 2025 as an online remote exam. That gave me about one month to prepare.

## My Preparation Strategy

Even though I had completed GCP training before, I quickly realized that there was still a gap between general GCP familiarity and the depth needed for the exam.

My background is more in medical devices and IVD, not traditional pharma clinical trials. Because of that, some terminology and concepts, especially around clinical trial design and statistical considerations, were not as familiar to me at the beginning.

The exam references multiple ICH and ethics-related documents. Instead of listing all of them here, I recommend checking the current ACRP exam preparation page directly before starting your study plan:

[ACRP Exam Preparation](https://acrpnet.org/certification/exam-preparation)

One important update to be aware of is the transition from ICH E6(R2) to ICH E6(R3). When I prepared for the exam, my study was still based on the materials listed for my exam window. However, beginning July 15, 2026, ICH E6(R3) Guideline for Good Clinical Practice will replace E6(R2) across all ACRP examinations. Because of this, always check the current ACRP reference list before preparing.

## Reading the Guidelines Is Necessary, But Not Enough

The ICH guidelines are important, but they are also very dry. If I only read them passively, I would miss many details.

What worked better for me was active reading. After each section, I asked myself:

- What is the key responsibility here?
- Who is responsible: sponsor, investigator, IRB/IEC, monitor, or regulatory authority?
- What should happen before enrollment?
- What should be documented?
- What is required versus what is recommended?
- How would this concept appear in a site monitoring or clinical operations scenario?


Another method that helped me was connecting the guidelines back to my own clinical research experience. When I read a section about protocol amendments, IRB approval, informed consent, monitoring, safety reporting, or essential documents, I tried to map it to situations I had seen or could imagine in real clinical study operations.

For example:

- If a protocol is revised before enrollment starts, what needs to happen before the first subject is enrolled?
- If a monitor identifies missing source documentation, what should be documented, communicated, and followed up?
- If a deviation occurs, how should the site document it, and how should the sponsor or monitor evaluate its impact?
- If informed consent was not properly completed before a study procedure, why is this more serious than a routine documentation issue?

Thinking this way made the guidelines less abstract. Instead of memorizing isolated rules, I tried to understand how each requirement protects human subjects, supports data integrity, and maintains trial quality.

If you already have clinical research experience, I recommend actively connecting your experience to the guideline sections you are reading. If you do not have much hands-on experience yet, practice questions can help create that context and guide your thinking.

## How I Thought About the Exam Questions

Based on my experience, the exam was not only about memorizing definitions. There were direct concept questions, but many questions were scenario-based. My rough feeling was that about 30% of the questions were closer to direct recall or definition-based questions, while a larger portion required applying GCP principles to practical situations.

For example, the exam may not simply ask, “What is the role of the IRB/IEC?” A more realistic question may describe a situation where a protocol was approved, then revised before enrollment began. The question may ask what the sponsor and investigator should do before enrolling the first subject. To answer correctly, you need to recognize that the revised protocol should receive appropriate IRB/IEC approval before implementation, rather than relying on the earlier approval.

That is why practice questions were very important for me. They helped me shift from “I read this guideline” to “I can apply this principle under exam pressure.”

## Practice Questions and Simulation Exam

I created a set of original practice questions while preparing for the exam. The goal was not to reproduce real exam questions, but to simulate the style of thinking needed for the exam: guideline-based, scenario-driven, and focused on choosing the best answer.

The practice set includes:

- Scenario-based questions
- Direct concept questions
- Answer keys
- Explanations for why the correct answer is preferred
- Questions organized around key ICH/GCP topics

## Exam Structure and Timing

The exam has 125 multiple-choice questions, and 25 of them are pre-test items that do not count toward the final score. However, the candidate does not know which questions are pre-test questions, so every question needs to be treated seriously.

The full exam time is three hours. Because of that, I recommend doing at least one full-length practice exam before test day. The purpose is not only to test your knowledge, but also to practice pacing, concentration, and decision-making under time pressure.

## What Happened on My First Exam Day

My first exam was scheduled as an online remote exam on September 27, 2025.

During the break, I left the room. I did not realize that this would cause an issue with the remote proctoring process. My exam was terminated. I immediately contacted PSI, and the issue was escalated to ACRP. After review, ACRP confirmed that I should have been able to leave during the break, because it was a break. However, the experience taught me an important lesson: with remote proctoring, you should communicate clearly with the proctor before doing anything.

Remote proctoring can be very strict. Even small movements may trigger warnings. For example, I tend to put my hand near my chin or mouth when I am concentrating, but during the remote exam this became distracting because the proctor would message me about it.

After that experience, ACRP allowed me to reschedule. They recommended an in-person exam, and I decided to take it at a nearby test center.

## My In-Person Exam Experience

I took the rescheduled exam on October 7, 2025 at 8:00 AM at a test center.

The in-person experience was much better for me. I arrived about 30 minutes early and provided two IDs. The test center provided the materials I needed, including a whiteboard, marker, and calculator.

One major difference between online and in-person testing was the exam flow.

For my online exam, the test was divided into two sections:

- First section: 90 minutes, 62 questions
- Break: 10 minutes
- Second section: 90 minutes, 63 questions

Once the first section was completed, I could not go back to it.

For the in-person exam, there was no scheduled break between two separate sections, but I could go to the restroom if needed. I could also review all 125 questions during the exam. I personally preferred this format because I could manage my own pacing and review flagged questions more flexibly.

The test center environment also helped me focus. There were fewer distractions, and I could use earplugs or headphones to reduce noise.

## Final Thoughts

Passing the CCRA exam was meaningful to me because it helped connect my sponsor-side clinical affairs work with the operational and monitoring principles that support clinical trial quality.

The preparation process was not easy. The materials were dry, and some topics were outside my original device-focused background. But the process made me more confident in the foundational knowledge, skills, and ethical standards expected of clinical monitors.

For anyone preparing for the exam, I hope the certification process helps you strengthen your foundation in clinical research and supports both your career development and your day-to-day work in clinical trial execution. Good luck with your preparation.
