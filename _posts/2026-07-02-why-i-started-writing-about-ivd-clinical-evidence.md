My career has not followed a straight line, but one theme has stayed consistent: I am interested in how scientific ideas become useful in the real world.

I was trained as a researcher in microbiology, environmental engineering, and antimicrobial resistance. In graduate school, I learned how to ask scientific questions, design experiments, interpret imperfect data, and work through uncertainty. I enjoyed the research process, especially the part where a complex biological question slowly becomes more structured through evidence.

After moving into the in vitro diagnostics industry, I started from the R&D side. I worked closer to assay development, verification and validation, technical documentation, and cross-functional product development. Over time, my work moved toward clinical and regulatory-facing projects. I became more involved in clinical study planning, protocol development, CRF design, site coordination, EDC/eTMF setup, and evidence generation for diagnostic products.

That transition changed how I think about diagnostics.

A diagnostic test is not only a technical product. It also needs to work in the intended use population, in real clinical settings, with real operators, real specimens, imperfect workflows, and practical constraints. A test can look promising analytically, but the clinical evidence determines whether it can support the claims it is meant to make.

This is why I have become increasingly interested in IVD clinical evidence.

Clinical evidence sits at the intersection of science, regulation, operations, and real-world use. It asks questions such as:

- What is the intended use of the test?
- What population should be enrolled?
- What comparator is appropriate?
- What specimens should be collected?
- What level of sensitivity and specificity is clinically meaningful?
- How should invalid, discordant, or missing results be handled?
- How does the study design support the final product claim?

These questions are practical, but they are also scientific. They require clear thinking, careful design, and an understanding of both the technology and the clinical context.

I started this website as a place to organize my learning and reflections. I plan to write about IVD clinical validation studies, diagnostic evidence generation, regulatory trends, FDA approval examples, CLIA waiver strategy, WHO prequalification, and selected case studies from the diagnostics field.

Some posts may be technical. Some may be career reflections. Some may simply be notes from trying to understand how a diagnostic product moves from development to clinical use.

My goal is not to present myself as an expert with all the answers. It is to build a clearer way of thinking about diagnostic evidence, one case and one question at a time.
